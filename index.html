Coptic In Kentucky

In the Fall of 2018, the library at The Southern Baptist Theological Seminary partnered with faculty for a seminar on Coptic. For the students, the aim of the seminar was to learn the grammar of Coptic and gain familiarity transcribing manuscripts, but for the library, the goal was to gain information on the unstudied fragments.

We partnered together by digitizing the fragments and making them available online via Google Drive. Students were then able to study the fragments and create transcriptions which will be used for digital editions and cataloging metadata

In the process of unearthing knowledge about this neglected collection, we have sought to engage the public and partner with local institutions.

History

Early Years

The Seminary has had a number of artifacts in its holdings for years.

The most well known is the mummy Sheryet-Mehyet (affectionately known as "Sherry").

Creation of Museums

The 1960's saw the creation of two museums at SBTS:

  • 1961 - The Eisenberg Museum
  • 1963 - The Nicol Museum

In time, the two museums were coalesced into one

  • 1991 - Joseph A. Callaway Archaeological Musuem

As interest in archaeology waned, part of the two collections was moved to a space in the library while the remainder went into storge.

Current State

There is a small museum space that displays a number of artifacts, but it has not been updated in years.

In November of 2016 a complete Torah Scroll was gifted to Seminary.

The majority of the collection sits in storage and data is scattered over various computers and storage boxes.

An inquiry into a Syriac manuscript prompted deeper explroation into the collection and the need to have it unified and available for use.

The first items to be studied were the Greek and Coptic papyri.

Tools

What has made this project especially difficult is the lack of funding.

Because of this, we have focused on using open source and collaborative tools, including:

  • GDrive - for sharing and editing data
  • Omeka - for cataloging artifacts
  • GIMP & XnViewMP - for image processing

Being able to share images and data in a collaborative way has been beneficial in allowing student to research objects while minimizing how often they are handled, this is especially useful for delicate papyri.

Research Areas

Inventorying & Cataloging

Inventorying and cataloging has been an ongoing process. Metadata from various sources has been compiled and brought up to Dublin Core standards. We use the Getty Art & Architecture Thesaurus for controlled vocabularly.

Digitizing

Images for many of the items exist but are of low quality and not in a digital format. Papyri have been scanned at archival quality levels, and processed for the web. Researchers from the University of Louisville have aided in identifying many unidentified or misidentified items, especially those related to our Coptic materials.

Donations

We have parternered with a researcher in Texas to unify the Tel Mishnaqag (Machaerus) collection which is housed at the Cobb Institute. Epigraphic pieces from this collection are being deciphered using multi-spectral imaging.

Seminars

Greek

The first seminar held was a papyrology class that focused on Greek papyri held in the library's collection, but not part of the artifacts collection. Graduate students worked on transcribing and interpreting these papyri, and presented their findings to faculty and students.

This provided invaluable cataloging metadata including the source of origin, a transcription, and a translation. One of the students' work was accepted into a peer-reviewed journal in the field of papyrology.

Coptic

The success of the Greek seminar prompted another course of similar nature

Though the Greek seminar was about papyrology, the Coptic seminar was about the language. Much of the format was the same, however, with student projects culminatigng in a transcription and translation.

By partnering with students and faculty, more scholarship has been done on the materials than could have ever been done by a single person. The seminars have benefited the students as well with some producing publications.

Undergraduate

Tours

Classes on ancient Near Eastern history and Biblical Hebrew have benefited as well.

Guided tours have proven well received by the student body.

Texts

The Torah Scroll has also been used to highlight phyiscal features of the text.

Not only were these annotations used on the physical object, but the same is accomplished through GDrive's image annotations. This has proven popular for undergraduate reading groups.

Public Engagement

A stronger understanding of the artifacts has led to greater public engagement with the materials. A local Jewish congregation enjoyed viewing our scroll collection.

Additional museum displays have been set up to showcase materials that have been in hiding.

Future Plans

The primary goal of this project is to make the objects available to the public. The avenues we intend to take are:

  • Online exhibits through Omeka
  • Making the Greek and Coptic texts available through papyri.info
  • Ensure that the related assets (e.g. scans of placards, photographs) are linked to items

And any input you can give!
